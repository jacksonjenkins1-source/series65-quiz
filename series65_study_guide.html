Section 1 Economics & Business Information ~15% of exam · ~20 questions
Monetary Policy & the Federal Reserve
Three tools of the Fed
Open market operations (primary tool) — buying/selling Treasury securities. Buying = money supply increases = rates fall. Selling = money supply decreases = rates rise.
Discount rate — rate the Fed charges banks for short-term loans. Lowering encourages borrowing.
Reserve requirements — minimum % of deposits banks must hold. Rarely changed.
Expansionary vs Contractionary Policy
Expansionary (dovish): lower rates, buy securities, reduce reserve requirements → stimulates growth, risks inflation.
Contractionary (hawkish): raise rates, sell securities, increase reserve requirements → slows inflation, risks recession.
Federal Funds Rate vs Discount Rate
Fed Funds Rate: rate banks charge each other for overnight lending. Set by open market operations.
Discount Rate: rate the Fed charges banks directly. Usually slightly higher than fed funds rate.
Economic Indicators
Leading, Lagging, and Coincident Indicators
TypeDefinitionExamples
LeadingPredict future economic directionBuilding permits, stock prices, consumer confidence, money supply
CoincidentMove with the economy in real timeGDP, personal income, industrial production
LaggingConfirm trends already in placeUnemployment, corporate profits, CPI, prime rate
GDP (Gross Domestic Product)
Total market value of all goods and services produced within a country in a given period. Two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth = recession.
GDP = Consumption + Investment + Government Spending + (Exports - Imports)
Inflation Measures
CPI (Consumer Price Index): measures price changes paid by urban consumers. Most common inflation measure.
PPI (Producer Price Index): measures price changes received by producers. Leading indicator of CPI.
Core inflation: excludes food and energy (volatile items).
Interest Rates & the Yield Curve
The Yield Curve
Normal (upward sloping): long-term rates > short-term rates. Signals healthy growth expectations.
Inverted (downward sloping): short-term rates > long-term rates. Historically predicts recession.
Flat: little difference across maturities. Economic uncertainty.
Bond Price / Interest Rate Relationship
Bond prices and interest rates move in opposite directions (inverse relationship). When rates rise, existing bond prices fall. When rates fall, existing bond prices rise.
Exam tip: this inverse relationship is one of the most tested concepts on the Series 65.
International Economics
Balance of Payments
Current account: trade in goods and services, investment income, transfer payments.
Capital/Financial account: investment flows, foreign direct investment, reserve changes.
Currency & Trade
Strong dollar → U.S. exports more expensive for foreign buyers (exports decrease) → imports cheaper for U.S. consumers (imports increase).
Weak dollar → U.S. exports cheaper (exports increase) → imports more expensive.

Section 2 Investment Vehicle Characteristics ~25% of exam · ~33 questions
Equity Securities
Common Stock vs Preferred Stock
Common stock
Voting rights
Residual claim on assets
Variable dividends (if declared)
Paid last in liquidation
Preferred stock
Generally no voting rights
Priority over common in dividends
Fixed dividend rate
Priority over common in liquidation
Rights & Warrants
Rights: short-term (weeks) privilege allowing existing shareholders to buy new shares at a discount before the public offering. Protects against dilution.
Warrants: long-term (years) option to buy shares at a set price. Often attached to bonds as a sweetener.
Debt Securities — Bonds
Key Bond Terms
Par value (face value): amount repaid at maturity. Usually $1,000.
Coupon rate: annual interest rate stated on bond. Fixed for the life of the bond.
Current yield: annual coupon / current price.
Yield to maturity (YTM): total return if held to maturity. Accounts for price paid, coupons, and principal repayment.
Current Yield = Annual Coupon Payment / Current Market Price
Bond Pricing Rules
Bond trading at par: price = face value. Coupon rate = current yield = YTM.
Bond trading at a premium: price > face value. Coupon rate > current yield > YTM.
Bond trading at a discount: price < face value. Coupon rate < current yield < YTM.
Types of Bonds
TypeKey Feature
Zero-couponNo periodic interest. Sold at deep discount, redeemed at par. Highest duration.
CallableIssuer can redeem early. Investor faces reinvestment risk. Higher yield to compensate.
ConvertibleCan be exchanged for common shares. Lower yield; offers equity upside.
TIPSPrincipal adjusts with CPI. Protects against inflation.
MunicipalIssued by state/local governments. Interest usually tax-exempt federally.
Duration
Measures a bond's price sensitivity to interest rate changes. Expressed in years.
Higher duration = greater price change per 1% rate move.
Zero-coupon bonds have the highest duration. Short-term bonds and floating-rate notes have low duration.
Price change % ≈ -Duration × Change in interest rates
Exam tip: duration increases with longer maturity and lower coupon. It decreases with higher coupon and higher yield.

Mutual Funds & ETFs
NAV Calculation
NAV = (Total Assets - Total Liabilities) / Shares Outstanding
Mutual funds price once daily at market close. ETFs trade continuously throughout the day at market prices.
Mutual Fund Share Classes
Class A: front-end load (paid at purchase). Lower ongoing expenses. Best for long-term investors.
Class B: back-end load (CDSC — contingent deferred sales charge, paid at sale). Converts to A shares after holding period.
Class C: level load. Higher annual 12b-1 fees. No conversion. Best for short-term.
ETF vs Mutual Fund vs Closed-End Fund
FeatureMutual FundETFClosed-End Fund
PricingOnce daily at NAVContinuous (market price)Continuous (market price)
SharesUnlimited (open-end)Created/redeemed by APsFixed number issued at IPO

12b-1 Fees
Annual fund charge for distribution and marketing. Maximum 1.00% annually. Funds charging 0.25% or less in 12b-1 fees may call themselves "no-load."
Options
Calls and Puts
Call option
Right to BUY at strike price
Buyer profits when price rises
Seller profits when price falls or stays flat
Put option
Right to SELL at strike price
Buyer profits when price falls
Seller profits when price rises or stays flat
Exam tip: buyers have rights; sellers have obligations. Buyers pay premiums; sellers receive premiums.
Options Strategies
Covered call: own stock + sell call. Generates income; caps upside.
Protective put: own stock + buy put. Insurance against price decline.
Straddle: buy call + put at same strike/expiry. Profits from large price move in either direction.
Annuities & Insurance Products
Fixed vs Variable Annuity
Fixed annuity
Guaranteed interest rate
Insurance company bears investment risk
Not a security (no registration required)
Variable annuity
Returns vary with sub-accounts
Investor bears investment risk
IS a security (must be registered)
Annuity Phases
Accumulation phase: investor makes contributions. Growth is tax-deferred.
Distribution (annuitization) phase: investor receives periodic payments. Payments are taxable as ordinary income on the earnings portion.
REITs & Alternative Investments
REIT Requirements
Must distribute at least 90% of taxable income. Must derive 75%+ of gross income from real estate activities. Must have 75%+ of assets in real estate. At least 100 shareholders.
Hedge Funds vs Mutual Funds
Hedge funds: private, limited to accredited investors, lightly regulated, can use leverage/shorts/derivatives, typically charge 2% management + 20% performance fee.
Mutual funds: registered, available to all investors, highly regulated, cannot use unlimited leverage.

Section 3 Client Investment Recommendations & Strategies ~30% of exam · ~39 questions
Risk Measures
Systematic vs Unsystematic Risk
Systematic (market) risk
Affects all securities
Cannot be diversified away
Measured by beta
Examples: recession, inflation, rate changes
Unsystematic (specific) risk
Affects one company or sector
Can be reduced through diversification
Examples: product recall, management scandal, lawsuit
Beta
Measures price sensitivity relative to the market (benchmark = 1.0).
Beta > 1.0: more volatile than market (aggressive).
Beta < 1.0: less volatile than market (defensive).
Beta = 0: uncorrelated with market (e.g. T-bills).
Negative beta: moves opposite to market (e.g. gold sometimes).
Standard Deviation
Measures total risk — the dispersion of returns around the average. Higher standard deviation = more volatile = more risk. Captures both upside and downside variability. Used in the Sharpe ratio denominator.
Performance Measures
The Three Risk-Adjusted Ratios
RatioFormulaRisk usedBest for
Sharpe(Rp - Rf) / σStandard deviation (total)Stand-alone portfolio evaluation
Treynor(Rp - Rf) / βBeta (systematic)One component of larger diversified portfolio
Jensen's AlphaRp - [Rf + β(Rm - Rf)]CAPM expected returnManager skill measurement
Rp = portfolio return, Rf = risk-free rate, σ = standard deviation, β = beta, Rm = market return
Alpha
Excess return above what CAPM predicts given the portfolio's beta. Positive alpha = manager added value. Negative alpha = manager underperformed on risk-adjusted basis.
Modern Portfolio Theory & Asset Allocation
Correlation & Diversification
Correlation ranges from -1.0 to +1.0. Combining assets with low or negative correlation reduces portfolio risk without proportionally reducing return. Perfect diversification is achieved theoretically at correlation = -1.0.
Portfolio Risk = f(individual asset risks AND correlations between assets)
Efficient Frontier & Optimal Portfolio
The efficient frontier plots all portfolios offering maximum return for a given risk level. The optimal portfolio lies on the frontier where the investor's risk tolerance intersects. Portfolios below the frontier are suboptimal.
CAPM
Expected Return = Rf + β × (Rm - Rf)
Rf = risk-free rate (e.g. T-bill). Rm = expected market return. (Rm - Rf) = market risk premium.
CAPM prices systematic risk only — it assumes unsystematic risk is diversified away.
Strategic vs Tactical Asset Allocation
Strategic: long-term target weights based on investor objectives. Rebalanced periodically back to targets. Passive approach.
Tactical: temporarily deviates from strategic targets to exploit short-term opportunities. Active approach. Returns to strategic targets over time.
Investment Strategies
Dollar-Cost Averaging
Investing a fixed dollar amount at regular intervals regardless of price. Automatically buys more shares when prices are low, fewer when high. Reduces average cost per share. Eliminates timing risk. Best for long-term investors.
Rebalancing
Restoring the portfolio to its target asset allocation after market movements cause drift. Involves selling outperforming assets (overweighted) and buying underperforming assets (underweighted). Enforces buy-low-sell-high discipline.
Tax-Loss Harvesting
Selling securities at a loss to offset capital gains. Losses can also offset up to $3,000 of ordinary income annually. Wash-sale rule: cannot repurchase the same or substantially identical security within 30 days before or after the sale.
Suitability & Client Profiles
Suitability Factors
Must consider: investment objectives (growth, income, capital preservation, speculation), risk tolerance, time horizon, liquidity needs, tax situation, financial situation, and any special circumstances.
Lifecycle Investing Guidelines
Life StageTypical Approach
Young investor (20s-30s)High equity allocation. Long horizon absorbs volatility. Growth focus.
Mid-career (40s-50s)Balanced allocation. Begin shifting toward moderate risk. Retirement saving peak.
Pre-retirement (55-65)Reduce equity. Increase bonds and income. Capital preservation becomes priority.
RetirementIncome-generating assets. Preserve capital. Some equity to combat inflation over 20-30 year retirement.

Section 4 Laws, Regulations & Guidelines ~30% of exam · ~39 questions
Key Federal Securities Laws
The Four Major Acts
ActWhat it governs
Securities Act of 1933Primary market — initial sale of securities. Requires registration and prospectus disclosure. "Truth in securities."
Securities Exchange Act of 1934Secondary market — ongoing trading. Established the SEC. Regulates broker-dealers, exchanges, and insider trading.
Investment Company Act of 1940Mutual funds, ETFs, closed-end funds, UITs. Requires registration and disclosure.
Investment Advisers Act of 1940Investment advisers. Requires registration (SEC or state), Form ADV, fiduciary duty.
Investment Adviser Registration
SEC vs State Registration Thresholds
AUMRegistration Required
Under $100MState registration (with state securities regulator)
$100M - $110MTransition zone — may register with either SEC or state
Over $110MSEC registration required
Exception: advisers with 15+ clients in a state may need to register with that state regardless of AUM.
Form ADV
Part 1: organizational and business information. Filed electronically with IARD system.
Part 2A (Brochure): written narrative describing services, fees, conflicts, investment strategies, disciplinary history. Must be delivered to clients.
Part 2B (Brochure Supplement): information about specific IARs who will advise the client.
Delivery rule: deliver Part 2 at least 48 hours before signing, OR at signing if client has 5-business-day right to terminate without penalty.
Investment Adviser vs Investment Adviser Representative (IAR)
Investment Adviser (IA): the firm. Registers with SEC or state.
Investment Adviser Representative (IAR): individual who provides advice on behalf of the IA. Registers with the state in each state where they have a place of business or more than 5 clients.
Fiduciary Duty & Ethics
Fiduciary vs Suitability Standard
Fiduciary (investment advisers)
Must act in client's best interest
Must disclose all material conflicts
Continuous obligation
Higher standard
Suitability (broker-dealers)
Must recommend suitable products
Can recommend higher-cost option if suitable
Transaction-by-transaction basis
Lower standard
Prohibited Practices
Churning: excessive trading to generate commissions.
Front-running: trading own account ahead of client orders.
Insider trading: trading on material non-public information.
Market manipulation: spreading false information to move prices.
Misrepresentation: false or misleading statements to clients.
Cherry-picking: allocating profitable trades to favored accounts.
Disclosure Requirements
Must disclose all material conflicts of interest. A fact is material if a reasonable investor would consider it important. Must disclose: compensation arrangements, outside business activities, disciplinary history, financial condition if relevant.
Exempt Transactions & Securities
Exempt Securities (need not register)
U.S. government and agency securities, municipal bonds, bank securities, non-profit organization securities, insurance contracts (fixed annuities), short-term commercial paper (under 9 months).
Exempt Transactions (sales that don't require registration)
Isolated non-issuer transactions (individual selling own shares), transactions between issuers and underwriters, private placements (Reg D — limited number of sophisticated investors), transactions with financial institutions, unsolicited customer orders.
Accredited Investor Definition
Individual with net worth over $1M (excluding primary residence) OR annual income exceeding $200K ($300K jointly) in each of the past 2 years with expectation of same in current year. Entities with $5M+ in assets. Can access unregistered private placements.
Recordkeeping & Compliance
Recordkeeping Requirements
Investment advisers must maintain records for 5 years (first 2 years in easily accessible place). Some records (e.g. articles of incorporation, partnership agreements) must be kept for the life of the firm plus 5 years.
Annual Compliance Review
SEC-registered advisers must review their compliance policies and procedures at least annually. The Chief Compliance Officer (CCO) oversees the program. Must be in writing and reasonably designed to prevent violations.
Custody Rule
If an investment adviser has custody of client assets, they must: maintain assets with a qualified custodian, notify clients of custodian details, have an independent surprise examination annually, and meet additional requirements if also serving as custodian.
Advisers who only have authority to debit fees from client accounts are generally NOT considered to have custody of those accounts.
Wrap Fees, ERISA & Special Topics
Wrap Fee Programs
Bundle advisory, brokerage, and administrative services under one annual fee (typically 1-3% of assets). Must deliver a special wrap fee brochure (Form ADV Part 2A Appendix 1) in addition to the standard brochure. May or may not be in client's best interest depending on trading frequency.
ERISA Overview
Employee Retirement Income Security Act governs private sector (not government) retirement plans. Key requirements: act solely in participants' best interest, diversify plan investments, follow plan documents, pay only reasonable expenses. Fiduciaries can be held personally liable for breaches.
Blue Sky Laws
State securities laws. Regulate the offer and sale of securities within each state. Operate alongside (not instead of) federal laws. States can impose additional requirements beyond federal minimums. Named for concern that some investments had "no more basis than so many feet of blue sky."
